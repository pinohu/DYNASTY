## 46.1 Trust Documents

Trust documents form the foundation of the Dynasty Empire's wealth preservation strategy. These documents create the legal structures that protect assets from creditors, minimize taxes, and ensure wealth passes to future generations according to your wishes.

### 46.1.1 Dynasty Trust Template

**The Constitution of Wealth**

The South Dakota Dynasty Trust is the primary vessel for multi-generational wealth preservation. This trust structure provides perpetual existence (365 years or forever), zero state income tax, superior asset protection, and complete privacy. It serves as the ultimate parent vault that holds and protects the Empire's most valuable assets.

**Template Location**: `empire-bible/templates/legal/trusts/dynasty-trust-template.md`

**Purpose and Strategic Role**

The Dynasty Trust is not just a legal document—it's the constitutional framework for your family's financial future. It accomplishes several critical objectives:

1. **Wealth Preservation**: Assets placed in the trust are protected from creditors, lawsuits, and divorces
2. **Tax Minimization**: Eliminates estate tax at each generation and avoids state income tax
3. **Perpetual Existence**: Can exist for 365 years or in perpetuity (depending on jurisdiction)
4. **Privacy Protection**: Trust information is not publicly disclosed
5. **Control Maintenance**: Through Trust Protector provisions, you maintain influence over trust administration

**Key Clauses Explained**

#### Perpetuity Provision

**Clause**: "This Trust shall continue for the maximum period permitted by law (365 years or forever)."

**Explanation**: This provision allows the trust to exist beyond the traditional rule against perpetuities, which typically limits trusts to 21 years after the death of a life in being. South Dakota has abolished the rule against perpetuities, allowing trusts to exist in perpetuity. This means your wealth can benefit unlimited generations without trust termination.

**Customization Considerations**:
- Verify the jurisdiction's perpetuities law (South Dakota allows perpetual trusts)
- Consider whether you want a specific termination date or perpetual existence
- Discuss with counsel whether perpetual trusts align with your family's goals

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Verify jurisdiction allows perpetual trusts
- [ ] Confirm perpetuities language with trust attorney
- [ ] Consider whether to include termination provisions for specific circumstances
- [ ] Document perpetuities period in trust agreement

#### Spendthrift Provisions

**Clause**: "No beneficiary may assign, pledge, or encumber their interest. No creditor may reach the assets."

**Explanation**: Spendthrift provisions protect trust assets from beneficiary creditors. Even if a beneficiary has debts, creditors cannot force distributions from the trust or attach the beneficiary's interest. This protection is crucial for protecting family wealth from business failures, lawsuits, divorces, and other creditor claims.

**How It Works**:
1. Beneficiary receives distributions at trustee's discretion
2. Beneficiary cannot sell or assign their interest
3. Creditors cannot force distributions
4. Creditors cannot attach the beneficiary's interest in the trust

**Customization Considerations**:
- Some states have exceptions for certain creditors (child support, alimony, taxes)
- Consider whether to allow voluntary assignments in specific circumstances
- Discuss with counsel whether spendthrift provisions are enforceable in your jurisdiction

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Verify spendthrift provisions are enforceable in trust jurisdiction
- [ ] Review exceptions to spendthrift protection (child support, taxes, etc.)
- [ ] Consider whether to allow voluntary assignments for estate planning
- [ ] Document spendthrift provisions clearly in trust agreement

#### Distribution Standard: HEMS

**Clause**: "Distributions shall be made for the health, education, maintenance, and support of Beneficiaries."

**Explanation**: The HEMS (Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support) standard is an objective, ascertainable standard that allows distributions while maintaining creditor protection and tax benefits. It provides flexibility for trustees to make distributions when needed while preventing excessive distributions that could deplete trust assets.

**HEMS Defined**:

**Health** includes:
- Medical, dental, and mental health expenses (including insurance premiums)
- Wellness and preventive care expenses
- Long-term care expenses
- Health-related travel expenses
- Alternative medicine and therapy expenses

**Education** includes:
- Tuition, fees, and expenses for primary, secondary, and post-secondary education
- Educational materials and supplies
- Study abroad programs
- Professional development and certification programs
- Educational travel and experiences
- Tutoring and educational support services

**Maintenance** includes:
- Reasonable housing expenses (rent, mortgage payments, property taxes, insurance, maintenance)
- Transportation expenses (vehicle purchase, maintenance, insurance, fuel)
- Food and clothing expenses
- Utilities and household expenses
- Personal care and grooming expenses

**Support** includes:
- Reasonable expenses necessary to maintain the Beneficiary's accustomed standard of living
- Expenses necessary to support the Beneficiary's dependents
- Emergency expenses
- Other expenses necessary for the Beneficiary's support and well-being

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider whether to expand HEMS to include "comfort" or "welfare" (less restrictive but may reduce creditor protection)
- Consider age-based distribution schedules (e.g., 1/3 at 25, 1/3 at 30, 1/3 at 35)
- Consider whether to allow distributions for business investments
- Discuss with counsel the tax implications of different distribution standards

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Define HEMS standard clearly in trust document
- [ ] Consider whether to include age-based distribution schedules
- [ ] Document examples of HEMS-qualified expenses
- [ ] Establish procedures for distribution requests
- [ ] Train trustees on HEMS interpretation

#### Trust Protector Provisions

**Clause**: "The Trust Protector shall have the power to remove and replace Trustees and veto distributions."

**Explanation**: The Trust Protector is a non-fiduciary role that provides oversight and flexibility without the administrative burden of being a trustee. Trust Protectors can modify trust terms (within limits), remove and replace trustees, veto distributions, and adapt the trust to changing circumstances.

**Common Trust Protector Powers**:
1. **Remove and Replace Trustees**: Can fire and hire trustees without court approval
2. **Veto Distributions**: Can prevent distributions that are inconsistent with settlor's intent
3. **Modify Trust Terms**: Can decant trust or modify terms to adapt to changing laws
4. **Change Governing Law**: Can move trust to different jurisdiction if beneficial
5. **Add or Remove Beneficiaries**: Can adjust beneficiary class (subject to limitations)
6. **Interpret Ambiguous Provisions**: Can resolve disputes about trust meaning

**Limitations on Trust Protector Powers**:
- Cannot make distributions to themselves
- Cannot modify charitable remainder provisions without court approval
- Cannot change Administrative Trustee without cause
- Cannot exercise powers that would cause adverse tax consequences
- Cannot exercise powers that would cause trust to be included in settlor's estate

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider who should serve as Trust Protector (family member, trusted advisor, corporate entity)
- Define powers clearly to avoid ambiguity
- Consider succession planning for Trust Protector
- Discuss with counsel tax implications of Trust Protector powers

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Select Trust Protector (consider independence requirements)
- [ ] Define Trust Protector powers clearly
- [ ] Establish limitations on Trust Protector powers
- [ ] Plan for Trust Protector succession
- [ ] Document Trust Protector compensation (if any)
- [ ] Review tax implications with counsel

#### Decanting Provisions

**Clause**: "The Trustee may pour over assets into a new trust with substantially similar terms to adapt to changing laws."

**Explanation**: Decanting allows trustees to move trust assets from an existing trust to a new trust with different (but substantially similar) terms. This provides flexibility to adapt to changing tax laws, family circumstances, or legal requirements without court approval.

**When Decanting is Useful**:
- Tax law changes require trust modifications
- Beneficiary circumstances change (substance abuse, divorce, etc.)
- Trust terms become outdated or inefficient
- Need to change trust jurisdiction
- Need to add or modify provisions

**Decanting Process**:
1. Trustee creates new trust with desired terms
2. Trustee transfers assets from old trust to new trust
3. Old trust is terminated
4. New trust continues with modified terms

**Customization Considerations**:
- Verify decanting is permitted in trust jurisdiction
- Define "substantially similar terms" to ensure flexibility
- Consider whether to require Trust Protector approval for decanting
- Discuss with counsel tax implications of decanting

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Verify decanting is permitted in trust jurisdiction
- [ ] Define decanting authority and procedures
- [ ] Consider whether to require Trust Protector approval
- [ ] Review tax implications with counsel
- [ ] Document decanting procedures in trust agreement

### 46.1.2 Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

**The Liquidity Engine**

The Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) is a specialized trust designed to hold life insurance policies outside your taxable estate. When properly structured, the ILIT eliminates estate tax on life insurance proceeds, provides creditor protection, and ensures death benefits are distributed according to your wishes.

**Template Location**: `empire-bible/templates/legal/trusts/ilit-template.md`

**Purpose and Strategic Role**

The ILIT serves several critical functions in the Dynasty Empire structure:

1. **Estate Tax Elimination**: Life insurance proceeds are removed from your estate, avoiding estate tax at your death
2. **Liquidity Provision**: Provides cash to pay estate taxes, fund buyouts, or provide for beneficiaries
3. **Creditor Protection**: Death benefits are protected from creditors of both the insured and beneficiaries
4. **Control Over Distribution**: You control how death benefits are distributed to beneficiaries
5. **Generation-Skipping Tax Planning**: Can be structured to avoid GST tax on death benefits

**When to Use an ILIT**

An ILIT is appropriate when:
- You own life insurance policies with significant death benefits ($1M+)
- You want to eliminate estate tax on life insurance proceeds
- You need liquidity to pay estate taxes or fund business buyouts
- You want to protect death benefits from creditors
- You want to control how death benefits are distributed

**Key Mechanics Explained**

#### Crummey Powers

**Mechanic**: Beneficiaries have a 30-day window to withdraw gifts (making them "present interest" gifts) to qualify for the annual exclusion.

**Explanation**: To qualify for the annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 per person in 2024, $36,000 per couple), gifts must be "present interest" gifts—meaning the beneficiary has the right to use the gift immediately. Life insurance premium payments are typically "future interest" gifts (the beneficiary doesn't receive anything until death), which don't qualify for the exclusion.

Crummey powers solve this by giving beneficiaries the right to withdraw the gift within a specified period (typically 30 days). This makes the gift a "present interest" gift, qualifying it for the annual exclusion, even though beneficiaries typically don't exercise the withdrawal right.

**How Crummey Powers Work**:

1. Grantor makes gift to ILIT (premium payment)
2. Trustee sends Crummey notice to beneficiaries within required timeframe
3. Beneficiaries have 30 days to withdraw their share of the gift
4. If beneficiaries don't withdraw, the gift remains in the trust
5. Trustee uses the gift to pay insurance premiums
6. Gift qualifies for annual exclusion ($18,000 per beneficiary in 2024)

**Crummey Notice Requirements**:
- Must be sent within required timeframe (typically within 30 days of contribution)
- Must inform beneficiaries of their withdrawal right
- Must specify the withdrawal period (typically 30 days)
- Must be documented and maintained in trust records
- Must be sent annually for each premium contribution

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider withdrawal period (30 days is standard, but can be adjusted)
- Consider whether to limit withdrawal rights to prevent abuse
- Discuss with counsel whether Crummey powers are necessary for your situation
- Review tax implications with tax advisor

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Structure Crummey withdrawal rights in trust document
- [ ] Establish Crummey notice procedures
- [ ] Create Crummey notice template
- [ ] Set up annual Crummey notice process
- [ ] Document all Crummey notices sent
- [ ] Review tax implications with tax advisor

#### No Incidents of Ownership

**Mechanic**: The Grantor must not retain any control over the policy to keep it out of the taxable estate.

**Explanation**: For life insurance to be excluded from your estate, you cannot retain any "incidents of ownership"—any right or power over the policy. If you retain incidents of ownership, the death benefit will be included in your estate for estate tax purposes, defeating the purpose of the ILIT.

**What Constitutes Incidents of Ownership**:
- Right to change beneficiaries
- Right to borrow against cash value
- Right to surrender the policy
- Right to assign the policy
- Right to revoke an assignment
- Right to pledge the policy as collateral
- Right to receive policy dividends
- Any other right that gives you control over the policy

**How to Avoid Incidents of Ownership**:
1. Transfer policy ownership to ILIT before funding
2. Do not retain any rights over the policy
3. Do not pay premiums directly (make gifts to trust, trustee pays premiums)
4. Do not borrow against policy cash value
5. Do not change beneficiaries directly (trustee does this)
6. Do not receive policy dividends directly

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Paying premiums directly to insurance company (must pay to trust)
- Retaining right to change beneficiaries
- Borrowing against policy cash value
- Receiving policy dividends directly
- Exercising any control over the policy

**Customization Considerations**:
- Ensure trust document prohibits grantor from retaining incidents of ownership
- Consider whether to allow grantor to make premium payments (must be structured as gifts)
- Discuss with counsel how to properly transfer policy ownership
- Review with tax advisor to ensure estate tax exclusion

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Transfer policy ownership to ILIT
- [ ] Remove grantor as policy owner
- [ ] Remove grantor as beneficiary
- [ ] Ensure grantor retains no incidents of ownership
- [ ] Structure premium payments as gifts to trust
- [ ] Document policy transfer
- [ ] Review with tax advisor for estate tax exclusion

**Complete ILIT Structure**

The ILIT template includes the following articles:

1. **Article I: Trust Creation** - Trust purpose, irrevocability, governing law
2. **Article II: Trustee** - Trustee selection, powers, compensation, liability
3. **Article III: Life Insurance Policies** - Policies held, additional policies, premium payments
4. **Article IV: Beneficiaries and Distributions** - Beneficiary designations, distribution provisions
5. **Article V: Crummey Powers** - Withdrawal rights, notice requirements, procedures
6. **Article VI: Trust Administration** - Accounting, tax elections, trustee compensation
7. **Article VII: General Provisions** - Governing law, amendment procedures, severability

**ILIT Formation Process**

1. **Select Trustee**: Choose independent trustee (cannot be grantor or insured)
2. **Create ILIT**: Execute ILIT agreement
3. **Obtain EIN**: Get EIN for ILIT
4. **Transfer Policy**: Transfer policy ownership from grantor to ILIT
5. **Change Beneficiary**: Change policy beneficiary to ILIT
6. **Make Initial Gift**: Make initial gift to ILIT for premium payment
7. **Send Crummey Notice**: Send Crummey notice to beneficiaries
8. **Pay Premium**: Trustee pays premium from trust assets
9. **Annual Process**: Repeat gift, notice, and premium payment annually

### 46.1.3 Crummey Trust Template

**The Education Fund**

Crummey Trusts are separate trusts created for each child or grandchild to hold annual exclusion gifts. These trusts allow you to make tax-free gifts up to the annual exclusion amount ($18,000 per beneficiary in 2024, $36,000 per couple) while maintaining control over how the gifts are used.

**Template Location**: `empire-bible/templates/legal/trusts/crummey-trust-template.md`

**Purpose and Strategic Role**

Crummey Trusts serve several important functions:

1. **Annual Exclusion Gifting**: Maximize use of annual gift tax exclusion
2. **Wealth Transfer**: Transfer wealth to children/grandchildren tax-free
3. **Asset Protection**: Protect gifts from beneficiary creditors
4. **Control Maintenance**: Maintain control over how gifts are used
5. **Education Funding**: Structure distributions for education expenses
6. **Future Merger**: Can merge into Dynasty Trust sub-trusts when beneficiaries reach certain ages

**When to Use Crummey Trusts**

Crummey Trusts are appropriate when:
- You want to make annual exclusion gifts to children/grandchildren
- You want to maintain control over how gifts are used
- You want to protect gifts from beneficiary creditors
- You want to structure distributions for education or other purposes
- You plan to merge trusts into Dynasty Trust sub-trusts later

**Key Features Explained**

#### Withdrawal Rights

**Feature**: Beneficiaries have the right to withdraw gifts within a specified period (typically 30 days).

**Explanation**: Withdrawal rights are essential for tax efficiency. They make gifts "present interest" gifts, qualifying them for the annual gift tax exclusion. Without withdrawal rights, gifts would be "future interest" gifts, which don't qualify for the exclusion.

**How Withdrawal Rights Work**:

1. Grantor makes gift to Crummey Trust
2. Trustee sends withdrawal notice to beneficiary
3. Beneficiary has 30 days to withdraw the gift
4. If beneficiary doesn't withdraw, gift remains in trust
5. Gift qualifies for annual exclusion
6. Trustee manages gift according to trust terms

**Withdrawal Right Limitations**:

Some trusts limit withdrawal rights to prevent abuse:
- Limit withdrawal to annual exclusion amount ($18,000 in 2024)
- Limit withdrawal period (typically 30 days)
- Limit number of withdrawals per year
- Require withdrawal requests in writing

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider whether to limit withdrawal rights
- Consider withdrawal period (30 days is standard)
- Consider whether to allow partial withdrawals
- Discuss with counsel tax implications of withdrawal rights

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Structure withdrawal rights in trust document
- [ ] Establish withdrawal notice procedures
- [ ] Create withdrawal notice template
- [ ] Set up annual withdrawal notice process
- [ ] Document all withdrawal notices sent
- [ ] Review tax implications with tax advisor

#### Age-Based Distributions

**Feature**: "1/3 at 25, 1/3 at 30, 1/3 at 35."

**Explanation**: Age-based distribution schedules provide a structured approach to distributing trust assets to beneficiaries. This allows beneficiaries to receive distributions at key life milestones while maintaining asset protection until those ages.

**Common Distribution Schedules**:

**Conservative Schedule** (Recommended for most situations):
- Age 25: 25% of trust assets
- Age 30: 50% of remaining assets
- Age 35: 100% of remaining assets

**Moderate Schedule**:
- Age 30: 50% of trust assets
- Age 35: 100% of remaining assets

**Aggressive Schedule** (For mature beneficiaries):
- Age 25: 100% of trust assets

**Custom Distribution Schedules**:
- Age 18: Education expenses only
- Age 21: 10% for education/business
- Age 25: 25% outright
- Age 30: 50% of remaining
- Age 35: 100% of remaining

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider beneficiary maturity and responsibility
- Consider family values and goals
- Consider whether to allow HEMS distributions before age-based distributions
- Consider whether to merge into Dynasty Trust sub-trusts instead of outright distributions
- Discuss with counsel tax implications of different schedules

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Determine appropriate distribution schedule for each beneficiary
- [ ] Document distribution schedule in trust document
- [ ] Consider whether to allow HEMS distributions before scheduled distributions
- [ ] Plan for distribution administration
- [ ] Review tax implications with tax advisor

## 46.2 Entity Documents

Entity documents govern the formation, operation, and administration of business entities within the Dynasty Empire. These documents define ownership, management, distributions, and transfer restrictions. Every entity—from the holding company to operating companies—requires properly drafted entity documents to ensure asset protection, tax efficiency, and operational clarity.

### 46.2.1 LLC Operating Agreements

**The Rulebook for Business**

Every LLC (HoldCo, MgmtCo, OpCo, PropCo) needs a tailored Operating Agreement. This document is the "constitution" of your LLC—it defines how the company operates, how decisions are made, how profits are distributed, and how ownership can be transferred. Without a proper Operating Agreement, your LLC is governed by default state law, which may not align with your strategic objectives.

**Template Location**: `empire-bible/templates/legal/entities/llc-operating-agreement-wyoming.md`

**Purpose and Strategic Role**

The Operating Agreement serves multiple critical functions:

1. **Asset Protection**: Defines charging order protection and transfer restrictions
2. **Control Structure**: Establishes manager-managed vs. member-managed structure
3. **Tax Efficiency**: Allows for special allocations and tax elections
4. **Operational Clarity**: Defines decision-making processes and procedures
5. **Dispute Prevention**: Provides mechanisms for resolving disputes
6. **Succession Planning**: Defines what happens upon death, disability, divorce, or dispute

**Why Wyoming LLCs**

Wyoming is the preferred jurisdiction for Dynasty Empire LLCs due to:
- Zero state income tax
- Strong asset protection (charging order protection)
- Low annual fees ($60/year)
- Privacy protections (no public disclosure of members)
- Flexible management structures
- Series LLC availability

**Key Provisions Explained**

#### Manager-Managed Structure

**Provision**: "The Company shall be manager-managed. Management authority is vested in the Manager, not the Members."

**Explanation**: Manager-managed LLCs centralize control in one or more managers, similar to a corporation's board of directors. This structure is essential for the Dynasty Empire because it allows the holding company (or you) to control operating companies without giving members operational control.

**Manager-Managed vs. Member-Managed**:

**Manager-Managed** (Recommended for Empire structure):
- Manager has authority to make operational decisions
- Members have limited voting rights (major decisions only)
- Centralized control in Manager
- Similar to corporate structure
- Better for asset protection
- Better for multi-member structures

**Member-Managed**:
- All members participate in management
- Decisions require member votes
- Decentralized control
- Better for small, closely-held businesses
- Less asset protection
- More operational complexity

**Customization Considerations**:
- Choose manager-managed for all Empire entities
- Designate HoldCo or you as Manager
- Define Manager powers clearly
- Establish procedures for Manager removal/replacement
- Consider whether to allow multiple Managers

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Choose manager-managed structure
- [ ] Designate Manager(s)
- [ ] Define Manager powers and limitations
- [ ] Establish Manager removal procedures
- [ ] Document Manager compensation (if any)
- [ ] Review with counsel for asset protection

#### Charging Order Protection

**Provision**: "A creditor's sole remedy against a Member's interest is a charging order. The creditor has no right to participate in management or force distributions."

**Explanation**: Charging order protection is one of the most powerful asset protection features of LLCs. When a member has personal debts, creditors cannot seize the member's LLC interest or force distributions. Instead, creditors can only obtain a "charging order"—a lien on distributions that the LLC may never make.

**How Charging Order Protection Works**:

1. Member has personal debt (lawsuit, business failure, etc.)
2. Creditor obtains judgment against member
3. Creditor seeks to collect from member's LLC interest
4. Court issues charging order (lien on distributions)
5. LLC is not required to make distributions
6. Creditor receives nothing unless LLC makes distributions
7. Creditor cannot participate in management
8. Creditor cannot force liquidation

**Asset Protection Benefits**:
- Creditors cannot reach LLC assets directly
- Creditors cannot force distributions
- Creditors cannot participate in management
- LLC can continue operating normally
- Other members are not affected

**Customization Considerations**:
- Verify charging order protection in your jurisdiction
- Consider whether to include charging order buyout provisions
- Discuss with counsel whether charging order protection is sufficient
- Review with counsel for maximum asset protection

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Verify charging order protection in jurisdiction
- [ ] Include charging order protection language in Operating Agreement
- [ ] Consider charging order buyout provisions
- [ ] Review with counsel for asset protection
- [ ] Document charging order protection in Operating Agreement

#### Transfer Restrictions

**Provision**: "No interest may be transferred without the Manager's consent."

**Explanation**: Transfer restrictions prevent unwanted partners from acquiring interests in your LLC. Without transfer restrictions, a member could sell their interest to anyone, potentially bringing in undesirable partners or creditors.

**Types of Transfer Restrictions**:

**Absolute Prohibition** (Strongest):
- No transfers allowed without Manager consent
- Applies to all transfers (sales, gifts, assignments)
- Maximum control and protection

**Permitted Transfers** (Flexible):
- Transfers to family members allowed (with notice)
- Transfers to trusts for family members allowed
- Transfers to entities controlled by member allowed
- Other transfers require Manager consent

**Right of First Refusal** (Balanced):
- Member must offer interest to LLC/other members first
- LLC/members have right to purchase at offered price
- If declined, member can transfer to third party
- Provides some flexibility while maintaining control

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider which transfers should be permitted
- Consider whether to allow transfers to family members/trusts
- Consider whether to include right of first refusal
- Discuss with counsel tax implications of transfer restrictions
- Review with counsel for asset protection

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Define transfer restrictions clearly
- [ ] Specify permitted transfers (if any)
- [ ] Establish transfer approval procedures
- [ ] Consider right of first refusal provisions
- [ ] Review with counsel for asset protection
- [ ] Review tax implications with tax advisor

#### Buy-Sell Provisions (The 4 Ds)

**Provision**: "Upon Death, Disability, Divorce, or Dispute, the LLC or other Members have the right to purchase the Member's interest."

**Explanation**: Buy-sell provisions define what happens when a "triggering event" occurs. These provisions ensure continuity of operations and prevent unwanted partners from acquiring interests through inheritance, divorce, or other means.

**The 4 Ds Explained**:

**Death**:
- Member dies
- Interest passes to estate/heirs
- LLC/other members have right to purchase interest
- Purchase price determined by valuation methodology
- Prevents unwanted heirs from becoming members

**Disability**:
- Member becomes disabled (unable to perform duties)
- Disability defined in Operating Agreement
- LLC/other members have right to purchase interest
- Ensures operational continuity

**Divorce**:
- Member divorces
- Spouse may claim interest in divorce settlement
- LLC/other members have right to purchase interest
- Prevents ex-spouse from becoming member

**Dispute**:
- Member dispute that cannot be resolved
- Deadlock or fundamental disagreement
- LLC/other members have right to purchase interest
- Provides exit mechanism for disputes

**Valuation Methodologies**:

**Fixed Price**:
- Price set in Operating Agreement
- Updated annually or as specified
- Simple but may not reflect true value

**Formula-Based**:
- Price calculated using formula (e.g., EBITDA multiple)
- Reflects current business value
- More complex but more accurate

**Appraisal**:
- Independent appraiser determines value
- Most accurate but most expensive
- Typically used for larger businesses

**Hybrid**:
- Combination of methods
- Formula for initial calculation
- Appraisal if parties disagree
- Balanced approach

**Customization Considerations**:
- Define triggering events clearly
- Establish valuation methodology
- Set payment terms (lump sum vs. installments)
- Consider insurance for buyouts (life insurance, disability insurance)
- Discuss with counsel tax implications of buy-sell provisions

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Define all triggering events (4 Ds)
- [ ] Establish valuation methodology
- [ ] Set payment terms and timelines
- [ ] Consider insurance for buyouts
- [ ] Review with counsel for enforceability
- [ ] Review tax implications with tax advisor
- [ ] Update valuations annually (if fixed price or formula)

### 46.2.3 Employment Agreements

**Protecting the IP**

Every employee of the Management Company (CXI LLC) must sign an Employment Agreement. This agreement protects the company's intellectual property, trade secrets, and client relationships while defining the employment relationship.

**Template Location**: `empire-bible/templates/legal/contracts/employment-agreement.md`

**Purpose and Strategic Role**

Employment Agreements serve multiple critical functions:

1. **IP Protection**: Ensures company owns all work product and intellectual property
2. **Confidentiality**: Protects trade secrets and proprietary information
3. **Non-Compete/Non-Solicit**: Protects client relationships and prevents competition
4. **Clarity**: Defines employment terms and expectations
5. **Liability Protection**: Limits company liability for employment disputes
6. **Dispute Resolution**: Provides mechanisms for resolving employment disputes

**When to Use Employment Agreements**

Employment Agreements are required for:
- All W-2 employees
- Key employees (especially those with access to IP or clients)
- Employees who create intellectual property
- Employees with client relationships
- Employees with access to trade secrets

**Critical Clauses Explained**

#### At-Will Employment

**Clause**: "Employment is at-will and may be terminated by either party at any time, with or without cause or notice."

**Explanation**: At-will employment means either the employer or employee can terminate the employment relationship at any time, for any reason (except illegal reasons), without notice. This provides maximum flexibility for the employer while still allowing employees to leave when they choose.

**At-Will vs. Contract Employment**:

**At-Will** (Recommended for most employees):
- Either party can terminate at any time
- No notice required
- No severance required (unless specified)
- Maximum flexibility
- Lower legal risk

**Contract Employment**:
- Employment for specified term
- Termination requires cause or breach
- May require notice or severance
- Less flexibility
- Higher legal risk

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider whether at-will is appropriate for all employees
- Consider whether to include severance provisions for key employees
- Review state law requirements (some states have exceptions to at-will)
- Discuss with counsel enforceability in your jurisdiction

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Include at-will language in Employment Agreement
- [ ] Verify at-will is enforceable in your state
- [ ] Consider severance provisions for key employees
- [ ] Review with counsel for enforceability
- [ ] Ensure all employees sign agreement

#### Confidentiality Provisions

**Clause**: "Employee agrees to maintain confidentiality of all proprietary information during and after employment."

**Explanation**: Confidentiality provisions protect trade secrets, proprietary information, and other confidential business information. These provisions survive termination of employment, providing ongoing protection.

**What is Protected**:
- Trade secrets
- Customer lists and information
- Pricing information
- Business strategies
- Financial information
- Technical information
- Marketing plans
- Any other proprietary information

**Confidentiality Obligations**:
- Maintain confidentiality during employment
- Maintain confidentiality after employment (perpetual)
- Not disclose to third parties
- Not use for personal benefit
- Return all confidential information upon termination

**Customization Considerations**:
- Define "confidential information" clearly
- Consider whether to include exceptions (publicly available information, etc.)
- Consider whether to include non-disparagement provisions
- Review with counsel for enforceability
- Ensure provisions survive termination

**Implementation Checklist**:

- [ ] Ensure all employees sign agreement

#### IP Assignment (Work Made for Hire)

**Clause**: "All work product created by Employee is 'work made for hire' and owned by the Company."

**Explanation**: IP assignment provisions ensure the company owns all intellectual property created by employees during employment. Without these provisions, employees may own the IP they create, even if created on company time using company resources.

**What is Assigned**:
- Inventions and patents
- Copyrights (software, content, designs)
- Trade secrets
- Trademarks
- Any other intellectual property
- Work product created during employment
- Work product created using company resources

**Assignment Requirements**:
- Must be in writing
- Must be signed by employee
- Must cover all IP created during employment
- Must cover IP created using company resources
- Should include future assignments

**Customization Considerations**:
- Define scope of assignment clearly
- Consider whether to include pre-employment inventions
- Consider whether to include post-employment inventions (if related to employment)
- Review with counsel for enforceability
- Ensure compliance with state law (some states have limitations)

#### Non-Solicit Provisions

**Clause**: "Employee agrees not to solicit clients or employees for 2 years after termination."

**Explanation**: Non-solicit provisions prevent former employees from stealing clients or employees after leaving. These provisions are generally more enforceable than non-compete provisions and provide important protection for the business.

**What is Prohibited**:
- Soliciting company clients
- Soliciting company employees
- Using company client information
- Interfering with company business relationships

**Duration and Scope**:
- Typically 1-2 years after termination
- Geographic scope (if applicable)
- Scope of prohibited activities
- Exceptions (if any)

**Non-Solicit vs. Non-Compete**:

**Non-Solicit** (More Enforceable):
- Prohibits soliciting clients/employees
- Does not prohibit competition
- Generally more enforceable
- Recommended for most employees

**Non-Compete** (Less Enforceable):
- Prohibits competition entirely
- More restrictive
- Less enforceable (varies by state)
- Use only when necessary

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider duration (1-2 years is typical)
- Consider geographic scope (if applicable)
- Consider scope of prohibited activities
- Review with counsel for enforceability in your state
- Ensure reasonableness (courts may not enforce overly broad provisions)

## 46.3 Real Estate Forms

Real estate forms govern the acquisition, leasing, and management of property assets within the Dynasty Empire. These documents are critical for PropCo operations and the OpCo/PropCo relationship structure.

### 46.3.1 Purchase Agreements

**Buying the Asset**

Purchase agreements (also called Purchase and Sale Agreements or PSAs) are used to acquire real estate assets for PropCo. These agreements define the terms of purchase, including price, contingencies, closing date, and property condition.

**Template Location**: `empire-bible/templates/legal/real-estate/purchase-agreement-residential.md`

**Purpose and Strategic Role**

Purchase agreements serve several critical functions:

1. **Legal Protection**: Creates binding contract for purchase
2. **Contingency Management**: Allows buyer to back out if conditions aren't met
3. **Due Diligence**: Provides time to inspect and investigate property
4. **Financing Protection**: Allows buyer to secure financing before closing
5. **Title Protection**: Ensures clear title before purchase
6. **Price Lock**: Locks in purchase price during due diligence period

**Types of Purchase Agreements**:

**Residential Purchase Agreement**:
- Used for single-family homes, condos, townhomes
- Standard residential contingencies
- Typically shorter due diligence periods
- More standardized terms

**Commercial Purchase Agreement**:
- Used for commercial properties, multi-family, office buildings
- More complex contingencies
- Longer due diligence periods
- More negotiation on terms

**Land Purchase Agreement**:
- Used for vacant land
- Focus on zoning, utilities, access
- Environmental considerations
- Development potential

**Key Contingencies Explained**

#### Inspection Contingency

**Contingency**: "Buyer has 30 days to conduct inspections. If Buyer is not satisfied, Buyer may terminate and receive earnest money refund."

**Explanation**: Inspection contingency allows buyer to inspect the property and back out if significant issues are discovered. This protects buyer from purchasing a property with hidden defects.

**What Inspections Cover**:
- Physical condition (structure, systems, appliances)
- Environmental issues (mold, asbestos, lead paint)
- Pest inspection (termites, etc.)
- Roof inspection
- HVAC inspection
- Plumbing and electrical
- Foundation and structural
- Septic/sewer (if applicable)
- Well water (if applicable)

**Inspection Process**:
1. Buyer hires qualified inspectors
2. Inspectors conduct inspections
3. Inspectors provide inspection reports
4. Buyer reviews reports
5. Buyer decides: accept, request repairs, or terminate

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider inspection period (30 days is standard)
- Consider whether to allow seller to cure defects
- Consider whether to allow price reduction for defects
- Review with counsel for enforceability

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Include inspection contingency in purchase agreement
- [ ] Set inspection period (typically 30 days)
- [ ] Define inspection standards
- [ ] Establish procedures for inspection requests
- [ ] Review with counsel for enforceability

#### Financing Contingency

**Contingency**: "Buyer's obligation is contingent upon obtaining financing within 45 days."

**Explanation**: Financing contingency allows buyer to back out if financing cannot be obtained. This protects buyer from being forced to close without financing.

**Financing Terms**:
- Loan amount
- Interest rate (maximum)
- Loan term
- Down payment
- Loan type (conventional, FHA, VA, etc.)

**Financing Process**:
1. Buyer applies for financing
2. Lender processes application
3. Lender conducts appraisal
4. Lender issues loan commitment (or denial)
5. Buyer decides: proceed or terminate

**Customization Considerations**:
- Consider financing period (45 days is standard)
- Consider whether to allow seller to see financing terms
- Consider whether to allow buyer to shop for better terms
- Review with counsel for enforceability

**Implementation Checklist**:
- [ ] Include financing contingency in purchase agreement
- [ ] Set financing period (typically 45 days)
- [ ] Define financing terms
- [ ] Establish procedures for financing updates
- [ ] Review with counsel for enforceability

#### Title Contingency

**Contingency**: "Seller shall provide marketable title free of liens and encumbrances."

**Explanation**: Title contingency ensures buyer receives clear title to the property. This protects buyer from purchasing property with title defects, liens, or other encumbrances.

**Title Requirements**:
- Clear title (no liens or encumbrances)
