#### Profit & Loss (P&L) Statement

**Purpose**: The P&L (also called Income Statement) shows revenue, expenses, and profitability over a specific period (monthly, quarterly, or annually). It answers the question: "Are we making money?"

**Template Location**: `resources/North_Star_Dashboard_v3.xlsx` (P&L tab)

**Standard Chart of Accounts Structure**

The Ohu Family Enterprise uses a unified Chart of Accounts across all OpCos to enable:
- Consolidated reporting
- Comparative analysis between OpCos
- Consistent financial intelligence
- Easier financial management

**Revenue Section**:

**Recurring Revenue**:
- Service contracts (monthly/annual)
- Subscription revenue
- Retainer fees
- Maintenance agreements
- Recurring revenue is the most valuableâ€”predictable and scalable

**One-Time Revenue**:
- Project-based revenue
- Installation fees
- Equipment sales
- One-time service calls
- Less predictable but often higher margin

**Revenue Recognition**:
- Recognize revenue when earned (not when received)
- For service businesses: recognize as services are performed
- For product sales: recognize when product is delivered
- Follow GAAP principles for revenue recognition

**Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Section**:

**Direct Labor**:
- Wages for employees directly providing services
- Technicians, installers, service providers
- Should be tracked separately from administrative labor
- Typically 30-50% of revenue for service businesses

**Materials and Supplies**:
- Parts and materials used in service delivery
- Equipment and tools consumed in service
- Inventory used in projects
- Should be tracked by job or project when possible

**Subcontractor Costs**:
- Costs paid to subcontractors for work performed
- Should be tracked separately from direct labor
- Important for understanding true service delivery costs

**Other Direct Costs**:
- Job-specific expenses
- Travel costs directly related to service delivery
- Equipment rental for specific jobs
- Any cost directly attributable to revenue generation

**Gross Profit Calculation**:
```
Gross Profit = Total Revenue - COGS
Gross Profit Margin = (Gross Profit / Total Revenue) Ã— 100%
```

**Target Gross Profit Margins**:
- Service businesses: 50-70%
- Product businesses: 30-50%
- Hybrid businesses: 40-60%

**Operating Expenses (OpEx) Section**:

**Sales and Marketing**:
- Advertising and marketing campaigns
- Sales team compensation
- Marketing materials and tools
- Lead generation costs
- Trade show and event expenses
- Digital marketing (SEO, PPC, social media)

**General and Administrative (G&A)**:
- Administrative salaries (non-direct labor)
- Office rent and utilities
- Insurance (general liability, workers comp, etc.)
- Professional services (legal, accounting, consulting)
- Software and technology subscriptions
- Office supplies and equipment
- Depreciation and amortization

**Management Fees**:
- Fees paid to CXI LLC for shared services
- Typically 5% of revenue or cost-plus structure
- Documented in Master Services Agreement

**Other Operating Expenses**:
- Bad debt expense
- Bank fees
- Miscellaneous expenses
- Any other operating costs

**EBITDA Calculation**:
```
EBITDA = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses
EBITDA Margin = (EBITDA / Total Revenue) Ã— 100%
```

**Target EBITDA Margins**:
- Service businesses: 15-25%
- Product businesses: 10-20%
- Hybrid businesses: 12-22%

**Net Income Calculation**:
```
Net Income = EBITDA - Interest Expense - Taxes - Depreciation/Amortization
Net Profit Margin = (Net Income / Total Revenue) Ã— 100%
```

#### Balance Sheet

**Purpose**: The Balance Sheet shows assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time. It answers the question: "What do we own and what do we owe?"

**Template Location**: `resources/North_Star_Dashboard_v3.xlsx` (Balance Sheet tab)

**Balance Sheet Equation**:
```
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

This equation must always balanceâ€”hence the name "Balance Sheet."

**Assets Section**:

**Current Assets** (assets expected to convert to cash within one year):

**Cash and Cash Equivalents**:
- Cash in checking accounts
- Cash in savings accounts
- Money market accounts
- Short-term investments (maturities < 1 year)
- Petty cash
- Target: 3-6 months of operating expenses

**Accounts Receivable (AR)**:
- Money owed by customers for services/products delivered
- Should be aged (current, 30 days, 60 days, 90+ days)
- Monitor Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
- Target DSO: < 45 days for service businesses

**Inventory**:
- Raw materials
- Work in progress
- Finished goods
- Supplies (if significant)
- Track inventory turnover
- Minimize inventory for service businesses

**Prepaid Expenses**:
- Insurance premiums paid in advance
- Rent paid in advance
- Subscriptions paid in advance
- Any expenses paid before they're incurred

**Other Current Assets**:
- Deposits
- Advances to employees
- Other short-term assets

**Fixed Assets** (long-term assets):

**Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E)**:
- Real estate (owned by PropCo, not OpCo)
- Vehicles
- Equipment and machinery
- Furniture and fixtures
- Computer equipment
- Less: Accumulated Depreciation
- Net Book Value = Cost - Accumulated Depreciation

**Intangible Assets**:
- Goodwill (from acquisitions)
- Trademarks and brand names (held by ToriMedia)
- Customer lists
- Non-compete agreements
- Less: Accumulated Amortization
- Net Book Value = Cost - Accumulated Amortization

**Other Assets**:
- Long-term investments
- Notes receivable (long-term)
- Other long-term assets

**Liabilities Section**:

**Current Liabilities** (obligations due within one year):

**Accounts Payable (AP)**:
- Money owed to vendors for goods/services received
- Should be tracked by vendor and due date
- Monitor Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)
- Target DPO: 30-45 days (balance cash flow with vendor relationships)

**Accrued Expenses**:
- Wages payable (accrued but not yet paid)
- Taxes payable (accrued but not yet paid)
- Interest payable
- Other accrued expenses

**Short-Term Debt**:
- Credit card balances
- Lines of credit (current portion)
- Short-term loans
- Current portion of long-term debt

**Deferred Revenue**:
- Customer deposits
- Prepaid service contracts
- Gift certificates
- Revenue received but not yet earned

**Other Current Liabilities**:
- Sales tax payable
- Payroll taxes payable
- Other short-term obligations

**Long-Term Liabilities** (obligations due after one year):

**Long-Term Debt**:
- SBA loans
- Bank term loans
- Seller notes
- Equipment financing
- Real estate mortgages (PropCo)
- Less: Current portion (shown in current liabilities)

**Other Long-Term Liabilities**:
- Deferred tax liabilities
- Other long-term obligations

**Equity Section**:

**Member Capital**:
- Initial capital contributions
- Additional capital contributions
- Capital distributions (negative)
- Represents owner investment in the business

**Retained Earnings**:
- Cumulative net income (or loss) over time
- Less: Distributions to owners
- Represents earnings retained in the business
- Starting retained earnings + Net Income - Distributions = Ending Retained Earnings

**Other Equity**:
- Treasury stock (if applicable)
- Other equity adjustments

**Key Balance Sheet Metrics**:

**Liquidity Metrics**:
- Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities (target: > 1.5)
- Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities (target: > 1.0)
- Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

**Leverage Metrics**:
- Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Total Equity (target: < 2.0)
- Debt-to-Assets Ratio = Total Debt / Total Assets (target: < 0.6)
- Equity Ratio = Total Equity / Total Assets (target: > 0.4)

**Efficiency Metrics**:
- Asset Turnover = Revenue / Total Assets
- Inventory Turnover = COGS / Average Inventory
- Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = (AR / Revenue) Ã— 365
- Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) = (AP / COGS) Ã— 365

#### Statement of Cash Flows

**Purpose**: The Statement of Cash Flows shows where cash came from and where it went during a period. It answers the question: "How did we generate and use cash?"

**Template Location**: `resources/North_Star_Dashboard_v3.xlsx` (Cash Flow tab)

**The Golden Rule**: "Profit is an opinion; Cash is a fact." We manage by the Cash Flow Statement because cash is what pays the bills, not profit.

**Cash Flow Categories**:

**Operating Activities** (cash from day-to-day operations):

**Cash Inflows**:
- Cash received from customers
- Interest received
- Other operating cash receipts

**Cash Outflows**:
- Cash paid to vendors
- Cash paid to employees
- Cash paid for operating expenses
- Interest paid
- Taxes paid
- Other operating cash payments

**Net Cash from Operating Activities**:
- Positive = generating cash from operations (good)
- Negative = consuming cash from operations (concerning if sustained)

**Investing Activities** (cash for long-term assets):

**Cash Outflows**:
- Purchase of equipment
- Purchase of vehicles
- Purchase of real estate
- Purchase of other businesses
- Capital expenditures (CapEx)

**Cash Inflows**:
- Sale of equipment
- Sale of vehicles
- Sale of real estate
- Sale of businesses
- Proceeds from investments

**Net Cash from Investing Activities**:
- Typically negative (investing in growth)
- Positive when selling assets

**Financing Activities** (cash from/for owners and creditors):

**Cash Inflows**:
- Proceeds from loans
- Capital contributions from owners
- Proceeds from equity issuance

**Cash Outflows**:
- Loan principal payments
- Distributions to owners
- Dividends paid
- Stock repurchases

**Net Cash from Financing Activities**:
- Positive when raising capital
- Negative when paying down debt or distributing to owners

**Net Change in Cash**:
```
Net Change in Cash = Operating Cash Flow + Investing Cash Flow + Financing Cash Flow
```

**Beginning Cash + Net Change in Cash = Ending Cash**

**Key Cash Flow Metrics**:

**Operating Cash Flow Metrics**:
- Operating Cash Flow (OCF)
- Operating Cash Flow Margin = OCF / Revenue
- Free Cash Flow = OCF - CapEx
- Cash Conversion Cycle = DSO + Inventory Days - DPO

**Liquidity Metrics**:
- Cash Runway = Cash / Monthly Burn Rate
- Months of Cash = Cash / Average Monthly Operating Expenses
- Cash Coverage Ratio = Cash / Current Liabilities

### 47.1.2 Budget Templates

#### Zero-Based Budgeting Methodology

**Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)** is a budgeting approach where every expense must be justified from zero, rather than using prior year's budget as a starting point. This ensures:
- No automatic budget increases
- Every expense is evaluated
- Resources are allocated efficiently
- Budgets reflect current priorities

**ZBB Process**:

1. **Start from Zero**: Begin with no assumptions about prior year's budget
2. **Justify Every Expense**: Each expense must be justified based on current needs
3. **Prioritize**: Rank expenses by importance and necessity
4. **Allocate Resources**: Allocate resources based on priorities
5. **Review and Approve**: Review budget with management and approve

**Revenue Budget Build**:

**Service Business Revenue Model**:
```
Total Revenue = Client Count Ã— Average Fee Ã— Retention Rate Ã— (1 + Growth Rate)
```

**Components**:

**Client Count**:
- Current active clients
- New clients expected (based on sales pipeline)
- Client churn (expected losses)
- Net new clients = New clients - Churned clients
- Ending client count = Beginning client count + Net new clients

**Average Fee**:
- Average monthly/annual fee per client
- May vary by client segment
- Consider fee increases
- Consider pricing changes

**Retention Rate**:
- Percentage of clients retained
- Target: > 90% for service businesses
- Calculate: (Clients Retained / Beginning Clients) Ã— 100%

**Growth Rate**:
- Expected revenue growth
- Based on market conditions
- Based on business strategy
- Typically 10-30% for growing businesses

### 47.2.2 NPV & IRR Models

**Purpose**: Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR) models evaluate long-term investments and acquisitions. They account for the time value of money and provide a comprehensive view of investment returns.

**Template Location**: `resources/North_Star_Dashboard_v3.xlsx` (NPV_IRR tab)

#### Net Present Value (NPV) Model

**Purpose**: NPV calculates the present value of future cash flows, accounting for the time value of money. It answers: "What is this investment worth today?"

**Formula**:
```
NPV = Î£ (Cash Flow in Year t / (1 + Discount Rate)^t) - Initial Investment
```

**Components**:

**Initial Investment**:
- Upfront cost of investment
- May include purchase price, transaction costs, working capital

**Cash Flows**:
- Annual cash flows from investment
- Should be after-tax cash flows
- Include all cash inflows and outflows
- Project for investment life (typically 5-10 years)

**Discount Rate (Hurdle Rate)**:
- Required rate of return
- Ohu Family Enterprise hurdle rate: 15%
- Accounts for risk and opportunity cost
- Higher risk = higher discount rate

**NPV Decision Rule**:
- NPV > 0: Investment creates value (do it)
- NPV = 0: Investment breaks even (marginal)
- NPV < 0: Investment destroys value (don't do it)

**NPV Calculation Example**:

**Scenario**: Business Acquisition
- Purchase price: $500,000
- Working capital: $50,000
- Initial investment: $550,000
- Annual cash flow (after-tax): $100,000
- Investment life: 10 years
- Discount rate: 15%

**Calculation**:
- Year 1-10: $100,000 per year
- Present value of cash flows: $501,880 (using PV formula)
- NPV = $501,880 - $550,000 = -$48,120

**Result**: NPV < 0, so this investment does not meet our hurdle rate. We would not proceed unless strategic value justifies the lower return.

This helps understand investment risk and key drivers.

#### Internal Rate of Return (IRR) Model

**Purpose**: IRR calculates the annualized return rate of an investment. It answers: "What is the effective annual return on this investment?"

**Formula**:
```
IRR is the discount rate where NPV = 0
Solve for r: 0 = Î£ (Cash Flow in Year t / (1 + r)^t) - Initial Investment
```

**IRR Decision Rule**:
- IRR > Hurdle Rate (15%): Investment creates value (do it)
- IRR = Hurdle Rate: Investment breaks even (marginal)
- IRR < Hurdle Rate: Investment destroys value (don't do it)

**IRR Calculation Example**:

**Scenario**: Same business acquisition as above
- Initial investment: $550,000
- Annual cash flow: $100,000
- Investment life: 10 years

**Calculation** (using Excel IRR function or financial calculator):
- IRR = 12.3%

**Result**: IRR (12.3%) < Hurdle Rate (15%), so this investment does not meet our return requirements.

**NPV vs. IRR**:

**When to Use NPV**:
- Comparing investments of different sizes
- When cash flows change sign (positive and negative)
- More reliable for decision-making

**When to Use IRR**:
- Comparing investments of similar size
- Understanding return percentage
- Easier to communicate to stakeholders

**Best Practice**: Use both NPV and IRR together for comprehensive analysis.

### 47.2.3 Break-Even Analysis

**Purpose**: Break-even analysis determines the minimum level of activity needed to cover all costs. It answers: "How much do we need to sell to break even?"

**Formula**:
```
Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs / (Price - Variable Cost per Unit)
Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs / (1 - Variable Cost %)
```

**Components**:

**Fixed Costs**:
- Costs that don't change with activity level
- Rent, salaries, insurance, etc.
- Must be covered regardless of sales volume

**Variable Costs**:
- Costs that change with activity level
- Materials, direct labor, commissions, etc.
- Expressed as cost per unit or % of revenue

**Price**:
- Selling price per unit
- For service businesses: average service fee

**Break-Even Analysis Example**:

- Fixed costs: $50,000/month
- Average service call price: $300
- Variable cost per service call: $150 (materials, labor)
- Contribution margin per call: $300 - $150 = $150

**Calculation**:
- Break-even service calls = $50,000 / $150 = 333 calls/month
- Break-even revenue = 333 Ã— $300 = $100,000/month

**Interpretation**: The business needs 333 service calls per month (or $100,000 in revenue) to break even. Any calls above 333 generate profit.

### 47.4.1 Property Analysis Model (The "Syndication" Model)

**For PropCo Acquisitions**

**Purpose**: The Property Analysis Model evaluates real estate investment opportunities for PropCo. It calculates key investment metrics and performs stress testing to assess risk.

**Template Location**: `resources/North_Star_Dashboard_v3.xlsx` (Property Analysis tab)

**Key Inputs**:

**Property Information**:
- Purchase price
- Rehab/renovation budget
- Total acquisition cost
- Property type (residential, commercial, multi-family)
- Location and market

**Income Information**:
- Current rent roll (existing tenants)
- Market rent rates
- Vacancy rate (current and market)
- Other income (parking, storage, etc.)
- Potential rent increases

**Expense Information**:
- Property taxes
- Insurance
- Maintenance and repairs
- Property management fees
- Utilities (if landlord pays)
- Other operating expenses

**Financing Information**:
- Loan amount
- Interest rate
- Loan term
- Amortization period
- Down payment

**Key Outputs**:

**Cash-on-Cash Return**:
```
Cash-on-Cash Return = (Net Operating Income - Debt Service) / Down Payment
```

**Components**:
- Net Operating Income (NOI) = Gross Income - Operating Expenses
- Debt Service = Principal + Interest payments
- Down Payment = Purchase Price - Loan Amount

**Target**: > 8-12% for residential, > 10-15% for commercial

**Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**:
```
DSCR = Net Operating Income / Debt Service
```

**Interpretation**:
- DSCR > 1.25: Strong (lenders prefer this)
- DSCR = 1.0: Break-even
- DSCR < 1.0: Negative cash flow (concerning)

**Target**: > 1.25 (preferred by lenders)

**Cap Rate**:
```
Cap Rate = Net Operating Income / Purchase Price
```

**Interpretation**:
- Higher cap rate = Higher return, higher risk
- Lower cap rate = Lower return, lower risk
- Market cap rates vary by location and property type

**Target**: Compare to market cap rates for similar properties

**Property Analysis Example**:

**Scenario**: Multi-family property acquisition
- Purchase price: $500,000
- Rehab budget: $50,000
- Total acquisition: $550,000
- Down payment (20%): $110,000
- Loan amount: $440,000
- Interest rate: 6%
- Loan term: 30 years
- Monthly debt service: $2,638

**Income**:
- Units: 4
- Rent per unit: $1,200/month
- Gross monthly income: $4,800
- Vacancy (5%): $240
- Effective gross income: $4,560
- Annual gross income: $54,720

**Expenses**:
- Property taxes: $6,000
- Insurance: $2,400
- Maintenance: $5,000
- Property management (8%): $4,378
- Other: $2,000
- Total expenses: $19,778

**NOI**:
- NOI = $54,720 - $19,778 = $34,942

**Metrics**:
- Cap rate = $34,942 / $550,000 = 6.4%
- Annual debt service = $2,638 Ã— 12 = $31,656
- Cash flow = $34,942 - $31,656 = $3,286
- Cash-on-cash return = $3,286 / $110,000 = 3.0%
- DSCR = $34,942 / $31,656 = 1.10

**Analysis**: 
- Cap rate (6.4%) is reasonable for market
- DSCR (1.10) is below target (1.25) - may need more down payment
- Cash-on-cash (3.0%) is low - may need to improve rents or reduce expenses

**Stress Testing**:

**Vacancy Stress Test**:
- Base case: 5% vacancy
- Stress case: 20% vacancy
- Impact on NOI, cash flow, DSCR

**Example**: 20% vacancy

### 47.4.2 Business Valuation Model

**What Are We Worth?**

**Purpose**: The Business Valuation Model calculates the value of Operating Companies. This helps track wealth creation, plan for exits, and make strategic decisions.

**Template Location**: `resources/North_Star_Dashboard_v3.xlsx` (Business Valuation tab)

**Valuation Methods**:

**Multiple of Earnings Method** (Most Common for Small Businesses):

**Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE) Multiple**:
- SDE = Net Income + Owner Salary + Non-recurring Expenses + Personal Expenses
- SDE Multiple: Based on market comps (typically 2.0-4.0x for service businesses)
- Business Value = SDE Ã— SDE Multiple

**EBITDA Multiple**:
- EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization
- EBITDA Multiple: Based on market comps (typically 3.0-6.0x)
- Business Value = EBITDA Ã— EBITDA Multiple

**Market Comparables**:
- Research recent sales of similar businesses
- Industry-specific multiples
- Location-specific adjustments
- Size-specific adjustments

**Growth Rate Adjustment**:

**Premium for Growth**:
- Businesses growing > 20% annually may command premium
- Premium: 10-30% above base multiple
- Justified by higher future earnings potential

**Penalty for Decline**:
- Businesses in decline may command discount
- Discount: 10-30% below base multiple
- Reflects risk and lower future earnings

**Business Valuation Example**:

**Scenario**: HVAC Service Business
- Net income: $200,000
- Owner salary: $100,000
- Non-recurring expenses: $20,000
- Personal expenses: $30,000
- SDE: $200,000 + $100,000 + $20,000 + $30,000 = $350,000
- Growth rate: 25% annually
- Market SDE multiple: 3.0x (for HVAC service businesses)
- Growth premium: 20% (for 25% growth)
- Adjusted multiple: 3.0 Ã— 1.20 = 3.6x
- Business value: $350,000 Ã— 3.6 = $1,260,000

**Alternative Calculation (EBITDA)**:
- EBITDA: $250,000
- Market EBITDA multiple: 4.0x
- Growth premium: 20%
- Adjusted multiple: 4.0 Ã— 1.20 = 4.8x
- Business value: $250,000 Ã— 4.8 = $1,200,000

**Average Value**: ($1,260,000 + $1,200,000) / 2 = $1,230,000

**Annual Valuation Process**:

**Purpose**: Value every OpCo annually to track wealth creation

**Process**:
1. Gather financial statements (P&L, Balance Sheet)

7. Track wealth creation
