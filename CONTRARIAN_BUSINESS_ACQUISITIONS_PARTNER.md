## **CODIE'S IDEAL BUSINESS FILTER**

This is the gatekeeper. Most deals don't make it through. That's how it should be.

The filter has seven gates. If a business fails any one of them, you pass. No exceptions. No "but this one's special." No "I can fix that later."

Here's the filter:

### **Gate 1: Revenue Under $10M**
Small businesses have less competition from institutional buyers, trade at lower multiples, and are easier to finance with seller notes or SBA loans.

### **Gate 2: Profitable Today**
You're not buying a turnaround project. You're buying cash flow. If it's not profitable now, it's not a businessâ€”it's a job with extra risk.

### **Gate 3: Net Margin â‰¥15%**
This ensures enough cushion for debt service, buyer income, operator salary, growth reserves, and the inevitable surprises. Below 15%, the margins are too thin.

### **Gate 4: Service-Based Business**
Service businesses are easier to understand, require less inventory and working capital, and scale through labor and processâ€”not complex supply chains or manufacturing infrastructure.

### **Gate 5: Fragmented Industry**
You want industries with thousands of small operators, not three dominant players. Fragmentation means less competition, easier bolt-on acquisition opportunities, and better pricing power locally.

### **Gate 6: Non-Proprietary, Not Founder-Dependent**
The business should be transferable. If it relies on the founder's unique genius, personal relationships, or proprietary technology, it's not a businessâ€”it's a personality cult. You're buying systems, not wizards.

### **Gate 7: Cash Flow Can Cover Everything**
After debt service, the business must generate enough cash to:
1. Pay you (the buyer) a meaningful salary
2. Pay an operator (if you're not running it full-time)
3. Fund growth reserves
4. Cover maintenance CapEx

If the math doesn't work, the deal doesn't work. Walk away.

## **SELLER FINANCING AVATAR: SCORING THE DEAL**

One of the most powerful levers in small business acquisition is **seller financing**â€”where the seller agrees to take part of the purchase price over time, rather than demanding all cash at close.

Seller financing reduces your capital requirements, improves your DSCR, signals seller confidence in the business, and often gets deals done that banks won't touch.

But not every seller will agree to it. That's where the **Seller Financing Avatar Score** comes in.

This is a 30-point scoring model that predicts how likely a seller is to offer financing. It's based on 14 factors that indicate motivation, flexibility, and financial need for an income stream.

### **The 14 Factors (Max 30 Points)**

1. **Long Operating History** (2 pts): Business has been around 10+ years. Stability signals less urgency to exit.

2. **Owner Tenure** (2 pts): Owner has been in the business 10+ years. Long tenure = emotional attachment, legacy concerns.

3. **Owner Age** (3 pts): Owner is 60+ years old. Retirement motivation is high.

4. **No Successor Alignment** (3 pts): No family member or key employee ready to take over. Seller has no internal exit option.

5. **Low Buyer Competition** (2 pts): Few buyers are circling. Seller has limited negotiating leverage.

6. **Brick-and-Mortar** (1 pt): Physical location ties the business to the community. Seller may want to see it continue.

7. **Messy Books** (2 pts): Financials are disorganized or incomplete. Banks won't lend, so seller financing becomes the only path.

8. **Overpriced vs. Balance Sheet** (2 pts): Asking price is 30%+ above asset value. Seller is emotionally attached and pricing high. You can negotiate down with creative structure.

9. **Unclean Financials** (2 pts): Personal expenses mixed in, inconsistent reporting. Seller knows a bank won't approve; seller financing is the workaround.

10. **No Leadership Team** (2 pts): Owner runs everything. Buyer will need time to transition, and seller financing aligns incentives.

11. **Some Distress** (2 pts): Health issues, burnout, partnership dispute. Seller wants out but needs income security.

12. **Relationship / Trust** (2 pts): You've built rapport. Seller likes you and wants to see the business in good hands.

13. **Prefers Income Stream** (2 pts): Seller doesn't want a lump sum (tax hit, reinvestment risk). Monthly payments are more attractive.

14. **Other Income Sources** (3 pts): Seller has rental income, pension, or other assets. Can afford to wait for payments.

### **Scoring Outcome**

- **0â€“11 points**: **Low** likelihood. Expect to need bank financing or cash.
- **12â€“20 points**: **Medium** likelihood. Seller financing is possible with the right structure.
- **21â€“30 points**: **High** likelihood. Lead with seller financing in your offer.

---

This score changes everything about how you approach the deal. If the score is high, you structure your offer around seller financing from the start. If it's low, you focus on bank financing and use seller financing as a fallback.

## **VALUATION & FINANCING MODEL**

Now we get into the numbers. This is where most buyers freeze up. Don't.

Valuing a small service business isn't complex. It's straightforward math, grounded in cash flow and industry norms.

- Owner's salary
- Owner's benefits (health insurance, car, travel, etc.)
- One-time expenses (lawsuit settlement, equipment purchase, etc.)
- Personal expenses run through the business (country club, family cell phones, etc.)

## **OFFER STRATEGY: THE THREE-OPTION MATRIX**

When you're ready to make an offer, don't present one number. Present three options.

### **Option B: Higher Price, Lower Cash + Seller Note**

- Rationale: "You get your full asking price, I reduce my risk, and you get a steady income stream."

## **DUE DILIGENCE: THE CHECKLIST THAT SAVES YOU**

Once you're under LOI, due diligence begins.

This is where you verify everything the seller told youâ€”and uncover everything they didn't.

Due diligence is not optional. It's the difference between buying a cash-flowing business and buying a lawsuit with employees.

Here's the complete checklist:

### **Financial Due Diligence**

- Last 3 years of tax returns
- Last 3 years of P&L statements
- Balance sheets (monthly or quarterly)
- Cash flow statements
- Bank statements (last 12 months)
- Accounts receivable aging
- Accounts payable aging
- Debt schedule (all loans, lines of credit, leases)
- Any off-balance-sheet liabilities

### **Customer Due Diligence**

- List of top 10 customers (revenue concentration)
- Customer contracts (if any)
- Customer churn rate
- Customer acquisition cost
- Average customer lifetime value
- Any pending disputes or complaints

### **Supplier Due Diligence**

- List of key vendors and suppliers
- Vendor contracts or agreements
- Payment terms
- Any exclusive relationships
- Backup suppliers (redundancy check)

### **HR Due Diligence**

- Organizational chart
- Employee list (names, roles, salaries, tenure)
- Payroll records (last 12 months)
- Employee handbook
- Any union agreements
- Turnover rate
- Any pending HR issues or complaints
- Key employee retention risk

### **Legal Due Diligence**

- Business licenses (current and valid)
- Permits (zoning, environmental, health, safety)
- Insurance policies (general liability, professional liability, workers' comp, property, etc.)
- Pending or past litigation
- Contracts (customer, vendor, lease, etc.)
- Intellectual property (trademarks, copyrights, domain names)
- Compliance with local, state, and federal regulations

### **Operations Due Diligence**

- Equipment list (age, condition, maintenance records)
- Facility tour (if brick-and-mortar)
- Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)
- Technology stack (software, systems, subscriptions)
- Inventory (if applicable)
- Scheduling and dispatch systems
- Job costing or project management systems
- Quality control processes

### **Market Due Diligence**

- Industry trends (growing, flat, declining?)
- Competitor analysis (who are they, what are they doing better?)
- Pricing positioning (premium, mid-market, discount?)
- Market saturation (room for growth?)
- Regulatory changes on the horizon

### **Assets & Liabilities**

- Real estate (owned or leased? Terms?)
- Equipment (owned, leased, financed?)
- Vehicles (owned, leased, financed?)
- Liens or encumbrances
- Environmental liabilities (especially for industrial businesses)

### **Transition Plan**

- Owner's transition timeline (30 days? 90 days? 6 months?)
- Owner's willingness to train and consult post-close
- Key customer introductions
- Key employee introductions
- Key vendor introductions

---

### **The Issue Log**

As you uncover problems, log them. For each issue, note:

- Description
- Severity (Low / Medium / High)
- Potential financial impact
- Proposed resolution
- Who's responsible (buyer, seller, third party)

Use the Issue Log to renegotiate price, adjust terms, or walk away if the issues are too severe.

---

### **The Closing Timeline**

Work backward from your target close date. Include:

- LOI signed
- Due diligence period (30â€“60 days typical)
- Financing approval
- Legal document drafting
- Final walkthrough
- Wire transfer
- Closing

Build in buffer time. Deals always take longer than expected.

## **FIRST 100 DAYS: YOUR INTEGRATION PLAYBOOK**

Congratulations. You closed the deal. You own the business.

Now comes the hardest part: not screwing it up.

The first 100 days are critical. This is where you stabilize operations, earn trust, identify quick wins, and set the foundation for growth.

Here's the plan:

---

### **Days 1â€“30: Stabilize and Listen**

**Goal**: Don't break anything. Build trust. Secure cash flow.

**Key Actions**:

- **Meet every employee** (one-on-one if possible). Learn names, roles, tenure, concerns.
- **Reassure customers**. Send a letter or email introducing yourself. Emphasize continuity.
- **Shadow the owner** (if they're staying for transition). Watch, listen, take notes.
- **Secure cash flow**. Ensure invoices are going out, receivables are being collected, payables are being paid.
- **Review financials weekly**. Compare actual performance to what was represented in due diligence.
- **Identify quick wins**. What's broken and easy to fix? (E.g., outdated website, no Google reviews, inefficient routing.)
- **Build relationships** with key customers, vendors, and employees.

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't make big changes yet.
- Don't fire anyone (unless they're actively stealing or incompetent).
- Don't rebrand or change pricing.
- Don't promise things you're not sure you can deliver.

Your job is to listen, stabilize, and earn trust.

---

### **Days 31â€“60: Systemize and Align**

**Goal**: Document processes, establish KPIs, clarify roles.

**Key Actions**:

- **Document SOPs** (Standard Operating Procedures). How do you acquire customers? Deliver service? Handle complaints? Process payroll? Write it down.
- **Establish KPIs** (Key Performance Indicators). Track weekly revenue, gross margin, customer acquisition cost, employee utilization, etc.
- **Build dashboards**. Create a simple one-page weekly report showing the numbers that matter.
- **Clarify roles and responsibilities**. Make sure everyone knows what they're accountable for.
- **Implement weekly team meetings**. 15â€“30 minutes. Review the week, celebrate wins, address issues.
- **Start training**. Cross-train employees so you're not dependent on one person.

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't over-complicate systems. Simple beats sophisticated.
- Don't micromanage. Delegate and verify, don't hover.

Your job is to bring order to chaos and create predictable processes.

### **Days 61â€“90: Optimize and Grow**

**Goal**: Improve margins, increase revenue, build momentum.

**Key Actions**:

- **Adjust pricing**. Look for underpriced services. Raise prices 5â€“10% on new customers first.
- **Renegotiate vendor contracts**. You're a new buyer. Vendors will often offer better terms to earn your loyalty.
- **Launch upsells**. What additional services can you offer existing customers? (E.g., plumber adds water heater maintenance plans.)
- **Improve marketing**. Update the website. Launch a Google reviews campaign. Start a referral program.
- **Improve routing and efficiency**. Can you reduce drive time? Optimize schedules? Reduce waste?
- **Add capacity**. Hire one more technician, salesperson, or admin if you're turning away work.

**What NOT to do**:
- Don't chase shiny objects. Stick to your core business.
- Don't over-expand. Grow methodically.

Your job is to build on the foundation you've stabilized and start generating returns.

## **GROWTH LEVERS: SIMPLE IMPROVEMENTS THAT COMPOUND**

### **1. Pricing Improvement**

Most small business owners underprice their services. They compete on price instead of value.

- HVAC: Annual maintenance plan ($200/year)
- Plumbing: Water heater inspection plan ($15/month)
- Landscaping: Monthly lawn care ($150/month)

**Expected Impact**: 10â€“30% of revenue becomes recurring within 12 months.

### **3. Upsells and Cross-Sells**

- Plumber fixing a leak? Offer to inspect the water heater.

**Expected Impact**: 10â€“20% revenue lift.

### **4. Reviews Campaign**

### **5. Website Improvements**

## **BOLT-ON ACQUISITIONS: COMPOUNDING YOUR RETURNS**

Once you've stabilized and grown your first acquisition, the next move is to layer on complementary businesses.

This is called a **bolt-on acquisition**, and it's one of the fastest ways to scale.

Here's how it works:

### **Step 1: Define Strategic Fit**

A bolt-on should:
- Serve the same customer base
- Operate in the same geography
- Offer complementary services
- Share overhead (admin, marketing, vehicles, etc.)

Example:
- You own a plumbing company. You acquire an HVAC company. Same customers, same trucks, same dispatch system.

### **Step 2: Source Complementary Businesses**

Use the same sourcing methods. Look for businesses with:
- Lower margins (you can improve them by integrating overhead)
- Weak marketing (you already have a customer list to cross-sell to)
- Owner burnout (less competition for the deal)

### **Step 3: Evaluate Cost Synergies**

What costs can you eliminate by combining operations?

Examples:
- Shared dispatch and admin staff
- Consolidated marketing budget
- Volume discounts with vendors
- Shared facilities or vehicles

### **Step 4: Evaluate Revenue Synergies**

What revenue can you unlock by cross-selling?

Examples:
- Plumbing customers need HVAC services
- HVAC customers need plumbing services
- Existing customer list = instant distribution for new service line

### **Step 5: Value Bolt-Ons at a Discount**

Because you can integrate and improve the business faster than a standalone buyer, you should pay less.

**Tactic**: Offer 2â€“2.5Ã— SDE for a bolt-on, even if standalone businesses trade at 3â€“3.5Ã—.

Justify it with synergies and speed.

### **Step 6: Finance It**

Options:
- Use cash flow from Business #1
- Seller financing (even more attractive for bolt-ons, since you have a track record)
- SBA expansion loan

### **Step 7: Integration Plan**

Within 90 days:
- Consolidate admin and dispatch
- Train teams to cross-sell
- Combine marketing efforts
- Standardize pricing and processes

### **Step 8: Measure Success**

Track:
- Combined revenue growth
- Combined EBITDA improvement
- Cost savings achieved
- Cross-sell conversion rate

---

**Expected Impact**: Bolt-ons can increase EBITDA by 30â€“100% within 12 months.
